Agriculture is the backbone of Sitia’s economy, with 32.6% of its land devoted to farming. The region’s mountainous and rocky terrains supports olive groves, citrus and nut trees, and vineyards, alongside cereals crops like wheat and barley. For livestock, climate change may increase disease risks, water shortages, and feed costs, undermining production efficiency. Adapting farming practices and improving resilience are essential to safe guarding Sitia’s agricultural heritage and rural livelihoods.

Sitia faces growing water challenges due to its dry climate, limited rainfall, and high demand from agriculture and tourism. Much of the water network is old and inefficient, with frequent leaks and salt build-up in pipes. Agriculture is the main water consumer, particularly olive groves and vineyards, while the key Sitia–Palaikastro aquifer is under stress from overuse and seawater intrusion.

Although Crete generally has sufficient water, Sitia often experiences summer shortages. Groundwater levels are declining, and several local aquifers shows signs of salinity and nitrate pollution from farming. Efforts are underway to improve irrigation efficiency and monitor water quality.

Sitia’s wastewater treatment plant serves about 20,000 people, using biological processes to protect the environment, but growing urban and tourism needs will require system upgrades.

Climate change amplifies existing vulnerabilities, with higher temperatures, longer droughts, and heavier rainfall expected to reduce groundwater recharge and worsen desertification. Rising sea levels could further degrade coastal aquifers, increasing costs for water extraction and treatment, and affecting agriculture, tourism, and residents’ quality of life.

Sitia’s UNESCO Geopark is one of the Mediterranean’s most unique natural environments, combining striking geological formations with exceptional biodiversity. Its dry, warm climate and location at the crossroads of Europe and Asia Minor have created habitats found nowhere else. The landscape includes aromatic shrublands, gorges rich in vegetation, and the famous Vai Palm Forest — the only natural palm forest in Europe, home to the rare Cretan date palm (Phoenix theophrastii).

The geopark hosts hundreds of plant species, many endemic to Crete and southeastern Aegean. Its gorges, such as Zakros and and Chochlakia, serve as refuges for rare flora including orchids, tulips, and wild herbs like thyme and oregano. Coastal zones support unique plant species and nesting grounds for sea lilies, further enriching Sitia’s ecological diversity.

The region’s fauna is equally diverse, featuring migratory birds, endangered raptors like the golden eagle and lammergeier, as well as mammals, reptiles, amphibians, and sea turtles. The nearby Dioysades Islands are internationally significant for hosting the world’s largest colony of Eleonora’s falcon and rare marine species such as dolphins and monk seals.

However, climate changes threatens this dedicate balance. Rising temperatures, prolonged droughts, reduced rainfall, and sea level rise could alter ecosystems, cause habitats loss, and increase invasive species and disease risks — endangering both biodiversity and the region’s ecological heritage.

The energy sector is a cornerstone of the Trentino’s economy, largely driven by renewable sources. Around 83% of Trentino’s electricity comes from renewables, with hydropower as the dominant source, producing over 4,300GWh annually. This output exceeds the province’s consumption by about 65%, allowing Trentino to export surplus green energy. Solar power and biomass-based co-generation also contribute, while fossil fuels are mainly used from transport and industrial heating.

Hydropower has deep historical roots in the region, transforming mountain rivers into vital energy sources since the late 19th century. Biomass remains important for heating, especially in domestic and district systems using wood and wood chips. However, the province still imports around two-thirds of its total energy needs, mainly oil and natural gas.

Tourism and industry are the most energy-demanding sectors, with electricity use in tourism rising significantly before the pandemic. The ski industry, central to Trentino’s winter economy, consumes large amounts of energy for lifts, snowmaking, and heating.

Climate change poses growing challenges, including shifts in heating and cooling demands and potential impacts on hydropower and infrastructure. To address this, Trentino’s 2030-2050 decarbonization plan emphasizes electrification, renewable expansion, and smarter energy management to achieve climate neutrality.

Water resources are one of Trentino’s greatest assets, shaping its economy, landscape and tourism identity. The province’s hydrographic system includes 9 major river basins, 297 alpine lakes, and over 10,000 springs, all influenced by its rugged Alpine terrain. To secure its future water resilience, Trentino is prioritising integrated, cross-sectoral water management, balancing hydropower, agriculture, and tourism needs while adapting to changing climate.

Trentino is one of Italy’s most environmentally rich regions, with 30,4% of its territory under protection — one of the highest rates in the country. All of Trentino’s protected areas have been awarded the European Charter for Sustainable Tourism (CETS), recognizing their commitment to responsible and high-quality tourism. These initiatives encourage collaboration among local communities, parks, and tourism operations to promote eco-friendly practices.

However, climate change poses growing risks — shifting vegetation zones, altering species distribution, and increasing the spread of invasive species and plant diseases. These pressures threaten biodiversity, freshwater ecosystems, and forest stability, underscoring the need for adaptative conservation strategies.

Norrbotten is one of Sweden’s most energy-intensive regions, driven by its cold climate, vast transport distances, and heavy industry. In 2018, total energy use reached about 31 terawatt-hours (TWh), with renewables making up roughly 50% of consumption. Most fossil fuel use comes from coal and coke in steel and iron production, particularly at SSAB in Luleå and LKAB in Kiruna.

The region is now leading Sweden’s green industrial transformation. Plans to replace coal-based steelmaking with hydrogen-based processes could eliminate nearly 10% of Sweden’s total CO₂ emissions, producing only water vapor instead of carbon dioxide. This shift will significantly reshape Norrbotten’s energy landscape and position it as a model for sustainable heavy industry.

Hydropower remains the backbone of Norrbotten’s electricity, accounting for about 90% of local production. Complementing this, the region is rapidly expanding its wind energy capacity. The Markbygden wind farm near Piteå, Europe’s largest, is expected to reach 12 TWh of annual output when completed in 2026. Solar energy is also growing, though still at small scale.

Electricity demand is projected to soar from 8 TWh in 2019 to over 100 TWh by 2050, driven largely by hydrogen production. Meeting this demand will require major expansion in renewable generation and infrastructure.

Norrbotten County, located in northern Sweden, spans from the Bothnian Bay coast to the alpine border with Norway, encompassing forests, wetlands, mountains, and river valleys. About 60% of the county is forested, mainly with Scots pine and Norway spruce, while 12% consists of mires that store large amounts of carbon and host specialized species. Norrbotten holds Sweden’s largest area of protected nature, with 2.6 million ha — about 25% of its total land area — under protection. Alpine habitats generally remain in good condition, while forest habitats show poor conservation status due to ongoing industrial forestry practices.

Norrbotten’s vast, diverse landscapes make it a vital region for Sweden’s biodiversity and climate resilience, highlighting the importance of sustainable land use and ecosystem protection.

Mining in Norrbotten continues to be a cornerstone of both regional and national industry, combining technological innovation with vast mineral resources to meet global demand for metals essential to modern manufacturing and green technologies.

The Region of Murcia is characterized by an arid climate and severe water limitations. Low rainfall, dependence on the Tajo-Segura water transfer since the 1980s, and intensive use of water for agriculture are the main causes of this situation.

Water scarcity particularly affects irrigated crops and domestic consumption. Water quality is also a major issue, with critical cases such as the Mar Menor, whose degradation is linked to agricultural discharges of fertilizers and pesticides.

Currently, water supply comes almost equally from surface and groundwater sources, putting aquifers at risk. Since 2008, the available volume has decreased, affecting both economic activities and household consumption. About 80% of water is allocated to domestic use, which manages to sustain its demand, especially during drought periods. However, water costs in Murcia are significantly higher than the national average: 25% more for consumption and 50% more for supply, although treatment costs remain similar.

Climate change, overexploitation, and pollution intensify this challenge. According to the Water Stress Index (WSI), Spain is under absolute water stress ( less than 500 m³/capita/year>), while Murcia has slightly improved since 2010 but remains a highly vulnerable region that requires well-defined strategies for sustainable water management.
